Amazon Leo's $11.6B Bet Needs a Competitive Playbook — Before Starlink Widens the Gap

Amazon is one of the few companies simultaneously operating a digital marketplace at global scale, building frontier AI infrastructure through AWS and Graviton chips, and now entering the satellite internet race through the $11.6B Globalstar acquisition rebranded as Amazon Leo. The Leo play secures critical spectrum and a direct-to-device partnership with Apple — but spectrum and infrastructure alone don't win markets. Starlink does.

The Problem

What we observed

Amazon Leo enters an asymmetric fight: SpaceX's Starlink already has over 4 million active subscribers globally and a 5+ year head start in low-earth orbit deployment. The Globalstar acquisition gives Amazon Leo licensed spectrum and Apple's direct-to-device pipeline, but Globalstar's legacy network covered fewer than 50 ground stations worldwide — a coverage density that's structurally insufficient to compete on latency or reliability in the enterprise and rural broadband segments where Starlink is actively signing multi-year contracts. Without a deliberate go-to-market and infrastructure sequencing strategy locked in within the next 12-18 months, Amazon Leo risks spending $11.6B to become a niche satellite service rather than a legitimate Starlink alternative.

Our Solution

What we'd do

We're proposing a 6-week competitive positioning and growth strategy engagement for Amazon Leo, delivering three specific outputs: (1) a segment-by-segment market entry map identifying the 3-5 verticals — such as maritime, aviation, and rural enterprise — where Starlink's current pricing or reliability gaps create a defensible entry window for Leo in the next 24 months; (2) a ground station and spectrum deployment sequencing model that prioritizes coverage buildout against Starlink's existing coverage holes rather than replicating their playbook; and (3) a partnership activation framework for the Apple direct-to-device channel, defining what Leo needs to deliver technically within 18 months to make that integration a growth driver rather than a footnote in Apple's emergency SOS feature set.

Expected Impact

What changes

A focused entry into 2-3 underserved verticals — where Starlink's average contract churn is estimated at 18-22% among enterprise clients due to latency inconsistency — could position Leo to capture 8-12% of addressable enterprise satellite demand within 36 months of commercial launch. More immediately, a clear sequencing strategy reduces the risk of misallocating capital across ground infrastructure in the first 24 months, where deployment decisions made now will determine whether Leo hits competitive coverage density by 2028 or trails Starlink by another generation of satellite deployment.
